Module 1
QuickBooks Online Essentials
Navigate QBO, understand the dashboard, and master the Chart of Accounts

What is QuickBooks Online?

QuickBooks Online (QBO) is cloud-based accounting software that lets businesses record transactions, manage cash flow, pay employees, and produce financial reports — all from a browser or mobile app.

Unlike desktop QuickBooks, QBO stores all data in the cloud, auto-saves changes, and can be accessed by multiple users simultaneously from anywhere.

QBO vs Xero — Key difference QBO uses "transaction forms" (invoices, bills, expenses) that feel like physical paperwork. Xero is more workflow-based. QBO is dominant in the US and Latin America; Xero is stronger in UK, Australia, and New Zealand.

The QBO dashboard

When you log in, QBO shows the Business Overview — a live summary of invoices owed, bills due, bank balances, and profit for the period. Understanding each panel helps you spot issues fast.

  • Invoices — money customers owe you (accounts receivable)
  • Expenses — what you've spent this period
  • Profit and Loss — snapshot P&L for the current month
  • Bank Accounts — current balances from connected feeds

The Chart of Accounts

Every transaction in QBO is coded to an account in the Chart of Accounts (CoA). The CoA is the backbone of your financial reporting — poorly set up CoA = unreliable reports.

Account TypeExamplesAppears On
AssetBank, Debtors, EquipmentBalance Sheet
LiabilityCredit Card, Loan, APBalance Sheet
EquityOwner's Equity, Retained EarningsBalance Sheet
IncomeSales, Services, Other IncomeProfit & Loss
ExpenseRent, Payroll, UtilitiesProfit & Loss
COGSMaterials, Direct LabourProfit & Loss (above GP line)
Golden rule Every transaction affects at least two accounts (double-entry). QBO handles the second entry automatically — but you must code the first correctly. Getting the account type wrong puts transactions in the wrong report.

Key navigation areas in QBO

  • + New — create any transaction (invoice, bill, expense, journal)
  • Sales — customer invoices, payments received, credits
  • Expenses — vendor bills, expense payments, vendor management
  • Banking — bank feed, reconciliation, bank accounts
  • Reports — P&L, Balance Sheet, Cash Flow, AR/AP Ageing
  • Payroll — employee management, payroll runs
  • Taxes — sales tax centre, payroll tax, VAT (for non-US)
🎯 Scenario — test your understanding
You open a QBO file for a new client and the Chart of Accounts has only 12 accounts — everything is coded to either "Income", "General Expenses", or "Other". The P&L is technically balanced but the client can't tell which expense category is largest or how profitable each service line is.
What is the fundamental problem and the correct first action?
A
The CoA is too simple — expand it with meaningful income and expense categories aligned to the business, then recode all existing transactions
B
Export to Excel and build the categories there — QBO reporting is limited
C
Add more bank accounts to separate the income streams
D
No action — the P&L balances so the accounts are correct
✅ A is correct. A flat, undifferentiated CoA produces useless management reports. The fix is to design a meaningful account structure and recode historic transactions. Without category-level detail, the P&L cannot drive business decisions.
Module 2
Customers & Sales
Invoices, payments, credits, and managing accounts receivable in QBO

The Sales Workflow in QBO

QBO's sales workflow follows a logical sequence from estimate to cash in the bank. Understanding the full flow prevents the most common AR errors.

Estimate
→ Invoice
→ Payment
→ Deposit

Creating an invoice

  • Go to + New → Invoice
  • Select the customer — QBO auto-fills their details
  • Add line items from Products & Services (or type a description)
  • Set the due date and payment terms
  • Apply tax rate if applicable
  • Save and send directly to the customer's email
Common mistake Entering a bank deposit directly without using Receive Payment. This records the cash but leaves the invoice open — your AR ageing then shows inflated overdue balances and your reports are wrong.

Receiving a payment

When a customer pays, always use Receive Payment (not a bank deposit) to close the invoice:

  1. Go to + New → Receive Payment
  2. Select the customer
  3. Tick the invoice(s) being paid
  4. Enter the amount received
  5. Choose to deposit to bank or Undeposited Funds

Undeposited Funds

Think of Undeposited Funds as a "cash drawer" — physical money or cheques you've collected but not yet taken to the bank. Use it when you batch multiple receipts into one bank deposit, so your QBO bank matches your actual bank statement perfectly.

Customer credits and refunds

SituationQBO ToolEffect
Customer overpaysOverpayment credit auto-createdSits on customer account; apply to future invoice
Goods returnedCredit MemoReduces AR; apply to open invoice or refund
Cash refund issuedRefund ReceiptRecords money out to customer; reduces bank
🎯 Scenario — test your understanding
A client calls to say they've paid three invoices with a single bank transfer of $4,750. The three invoices are: $1,800 (30 days old), $2,100 (15 days old), and $850 (5 days old). Your QBO shows all three as unpaid.
What is the correct QBO action?
A
Receive Payment → select customer → tick all three invoices → enter $4,750 → deposit to bank
B
Create three separate Receive Payment entries, one for each invoice amount
C
Record a bank deposit of $4,750 and code to Accounts Receivable
D
Mark each invoice as paid individually in the invoice list
✅ A is correct. QBO's Receive Payment screen allows you to apply a single payment across multiple invoices. This matches your bank statement (one deposit) while correctly closing all three AR balances. Creating three separate payments would inflate the bank by $4,750 × 3 = $14,250.
Module 3
Vendors & Expenses
Bills, expense payments, vendor credits, and accounts payable management

Bills vs Expenses in QBO

This is one of the most common sources of error in QBO. The choice between a Bill and an Expense determines whether a transaction appears in Accounts Payable.

FeatureBillExpense
Use whenYou'll pay later (on credit)You pay immediately
Appears in AP?Yes — shows in AP ageingNo — paid immediately
WorkflowEnter bill → Pay bill laterOne step — record & done
Vendor trackingFull vendor recordCan be vendor or ad-hoc
Rule of thumb If the vendor sends you a statement of what you owe, use Bills. If you hand over cash/card at point of purchase, use Expense. Using Expenses for credit purchases hides what you owe vendors and produces wrong AP reports.

Paying a bill

  1. Go to Expenses → Vendors → select vendor
  2. Find the open bill and click Make Payment
  3. QBO records the payment, reduces the bank balance, and closes the bill

Or use + New → Pay Bills to pay multiple vendors in one session.

Vendor credits

When a supplier issues a credit note (for returns, overcharges, or disputes):

  1. + New → Vendor Credit
  2. Select the vendor and enter the credit amount and account
  3. When paying future bills, QBO will offer to apply the credit — reducing the payment needed

Recording petty cash

Petty cash purchases are recorded as Expenses in QBO, using the petty cash bank account as the payment account. Keep a physical log of petty cash receipts and reconcile the physical cash to QBO's petty cash balance monthly.

1099 contractor tracking (US)

For US businesses: if a contractor is paid $600 or more in a year, a 1099-NEC must be issued. In QBO:

  • Set the vendor as a 1099 contractor in their vendor profile
  • QBO tracks all payments to that vendor throughout the year
  • Use the 1099 Wizard at year-end to prepare and file forms
🎯 Scenario — test your understanding
Your client receives an electricity bill for $340, a $1,200 monthly rent invoice, and pays $45 cash from petty cash for office coffee. All three are entered as Expenses rather than the correct transaction types.
Which of the three should be a Bill (not Expense), and what problem does this create?
A
The electricity bill ($340) and rent invoice ($1,200) should be Bills — entering them as Expenses means they don't appear in AP and the vendor balances show $0 owed when $1,540 is actually outstanding
B
All three should be Bills — even petty cash purchases create AP obligations
C
Only the rent invoice should be a Bill — electricity can be Expense because it's a utility
D
None — Expense is correct for all three as cash is always paid eventually
✅ A is correct. The electricity bill and rent invoice are credit purchases — money is owed to the vendor. They should be Bills so they appear in AP ageing. The $45 petty cash is an immediate cash purchase — Expense is correct for that one.
Module 4
Bank Feeds & Reconciliation
Connect bank feeds, categorise transactions, and reconcile accounts monthly

How QBO Bank Feeds Work

When you connect a bank account to QBO, transactions import automatically into the For Review queue. QBO uses machine learning to suggest categories based on past behaviour — but every suggestion must be reviewed before it is accepted.

Critical point Auto-accept without review creates miscoding errors. QBO's suggestions are pattern-based and frequently wrong for unusual or new transaction types. Review every transaction before clicking "Add" or "Match".

The three actions in For Review

ActionWhen to useWhat it does
MatchA QBO record already exists for this transactionLinks the bank line to the existing record — no new entry
AddNo QBO record exists — new transactionCreates a new transaction from the bank line
ExcludeTransaction is personal or irrelevantRemoves from feed without creating a record

The bank reconciliation process

Reconciliation proves your QBO records match the bank statement exactly. Do this monthly — never let it slip more than 30 days.

  1. Go to Accounting → Reconcile
  2. Select the account and enter the statement closing date and closing balance
  3. Match transactions in QBO to the bank statement line by line
  4. The "Difference" must reach $0.00 to complete reconciliation
  5. Click Finish Now — QBO locks the reconciled transactions

Common reconciliation differences and causes

  • Outstanding cheques — written in QBO but not yet cleared the bank (legitimate timing)
  • Deposits in transit — recorded in QBO but not yet credited by the bank
  • Bank fees not in QBO — add them directly from the reconciliation screen
  • Duplicate transactions — entered manually AND imported via feed
  • Wrong amounts — a common data-entry error; trace to the specific transaction
Best practice Always enter your opening bank balance correctly in QBO. Every reconciliation from then on builds on this foundation. A wrong opening balance creates a permanent difference that is hard to trace later.
🎯 Scenario — test your understanding
You're reconciling QBO for March. The bank statement shows a closing balance of $18,450. QBO's reconciliation screen shows a difference of -$240 after matching all visible transactions. You've checked all March transactions and they appear correct.
What is the most likely cause and the correct next step?
A
A transaction of $240 was entered in QBO in March but hasn't cleared the bank yet (outstanding cheque or deposit in transit) — check the list of uncleared items in the reconciliation and verify if they appear on the April statement
B
Post a $240 journal entry to a reconciliation difference account to force balance
C
Click Finish Now — a small difference under $500 is acceptable
D
Delete and re-import all March bank feed transactions
✅ A is correct. A consistent small difference is almost always an outstanding item — a cheque written but not yet presented, or a deposit not yet credited. Check the list of uncleared items. Posting a journal to force balance hides the real issue and creates a permanent record problem.
Module 5
Reporting & Analysis
Key QBO reports, what they tell you, and how to use them

The Core QBO Reports

Profit and Loss (Income Statement)

Shows income minus expenses for a period. The most-used report for business owners. Key questions it answers: Did we make money? Where are our costs highest? Which products/services are most profitable?

Cash vs Accrual toggle QBO lets you switch any report between cash and accrual basis. Cash basis shows money in/out only. Accrual includes unpaid invoices in income and unpaid bills in expenses — more accurate for business decisions.

Balance Sheet

A snapshot of what the business owns (assets), owes (liabilities), and the owner's stake (equity) at a specific date. The equation must always balance: Assets = Liabilities + Equity

Use it to monitor: cash position, debtor balances, outstanding loans, and net worth of the business.

Accounts Receivable Ageing

Shows all unpaid customer invoices grouped by age (current, 1-30 days, 31-60 days, 61-90 days, 90+ days). Your primary tool for managing debtors and prioritising collections.

Accounts Payable Ageing

Same concept for what you owe vendors. Helps manage cash flow by showing which bills are due soon and which are overdue — protecting your supplier relationships.

Cash Flow Statement

Shows cash in and out across three categories: operating activities (day-to-day business), investing (buying/selling assets), and financing (loans, equity). Explains why profit and cash don't always match.

Report customisation in QBO

  • Filter by date range, customer, vendor, class, or location
  • Compare periods (this year vs last year, this month vs budget)
  • Drill down on any number to see the underlying transactions
  • Save customised reports to the Reports menu for quick access
  • Schedule automatic email delivery of reports to management
Business QuestionQBO Report
Did we make a profit this month?Profit and Loss
Which customers owe us money?AR Ageing Detail
What do we owe suppliers?AP Ageing Summary
How much cash do we have?Balance Sheet / Bank Summary
Why is profit high but cash low?Cash Flow Statement
Which product line is most profitable?P&L by Class
🎯 Scenario — test your understanding
A business owner runs the QBO Profit and Loss report for the year and sees net profit of $95,000. They are confused — their bank account only has $8,200 in it. They ask you: "Where did all the money go? Is something wrong?"
What is the most accurate explanation?
A
Profit and cash are different things. The P&L includes income earned but not yet collected (debtors), and excludes loan repayments and capital spending that reduced the bank. Run the Cash Flow Statement and AR Ageing to explain the difference.
B
QBO has a calculation error — the P&L and bank should always match at year-end
C
The bank figure is wrong — re-import the bank feed for the full year
D
The P&L is on accrual basis — switch to cash basis and the figures will align with the bank
✅ A is correct. Profit ≠ cash. Accrual P&L includes sales invoiced but not yet paid (in AR, not yet in the bank), and excludes loan repayments and owner drawings (which reduce cash but not profit). The Cash Flow Statement bridges the two figures.
Module 6
Tax & Compliance
Sales tax, payroll tax, GST/VAT, and 1099 reporting in QBO

Tax in QuickBooks Online

QBO has a built-in Tax Centre that tracks tax collected and paid, and helps you prepare returns. The system is region-configurable — US users see sales tax and payroll tax; international users see GST or VAT.

Sales Tax (US) / GST (AU/NZ/CA) / VAT (UK/EU)

Regardless of region, the mechanism is similar:

  • Set up your applicable tax rates in the Tax Centre
  • Apply the correct rate to each invoice or bill line item
  • QBO calculates and tracks: tax collected (liability) and tax paid on purchases (input credit)
  • At filing time, QBO produces a summary showing net tax owed or refundable
Region note Your LedgerLearn region setting determines the tax terminology and rates in your assessment. SA users see VAT at 15%, Australian users see GST at 10%, US users see state sales tax at variable rates.

Payroll tax compliance (US)

1099 reporting

Any contractor (non-employee individual or LLC) paid $600 or more in a calendar year requires a 1099-NEC by January 31 of the following year. QBO's 1099 Wizard at year-end tracks all qualifying payments and prepares the forms.

Year-end QBO checklist

  • ✓ All bank accounts reconciled to year-end date
  • ✓ All outstanding bills and invoices reviewed (delete or write off uncollectible ones)
  • ✓ Payroll tax deposits confirmed as filed and paid
  • ✓ Sales tax return filed for final period
  • ✓ 1099s prepared for all qualifying contractors
  • ✓ Fixed asset depreciation journals posted
  • ✓ Prepayments and accruals reviewed
  • ✓ Year-end lock date set in QBO
🎯 Final scenario — putting it all together
At year-end review you discover: (1) Three customer invoices totalling $12,000 have been outstanding for over 200 days with no payment activity. (2) A contractor was paid $4,800 over the year but was not set up as a 1099 vendor. (3) The petty cash account shows a $180 balance in QBO but physical cash is only $65.
What are the three correct corrective actions in order of urgency?
A
(1) Write off the $12,000 as bad debt expense with credit memos; (2) Update the contractor to 1099 status immediately — even though under $600 threshold the record should be correct; (3) Post a $115 petty cash discrepancy to Shrinkage/Miscellaneous expense and investigate cause
B
(1) Delete the old invoices to clean the AR; (2) No action needed — 1099 is only for $600+; (3) Top up petty cash with personal funds
C
All three issues can wait until the external accountant reviews the file at year-end
D
(1) Send payment reminder to customers; (2) Issue the 1099 immediately regardless of timing; (3) Accept the discrepancy
✅ A is correct. (1) Invoices over 200 days with no activity are likely bad debts — write off using credit memos to Bad Debt Expense. (2) The contractor is under $600 so no 1099 is legally required, but good record practice means keeping their vendor profile accurate. (3) The petty cash shortfall ($115) must be expensed and investigated — someone may need to account for it.
